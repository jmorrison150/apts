One Dot = One Apartment
 Color = Price Per Bedroom

Once a month for a year, apartment listings were downloaded from padmapper in selected cities. For each apartment, one dot was placed on the map in that location and colored based on price per bedroom. The result is a fine grained snapshot of residents in transition. It looks much like a population density mapping, but tells us more about where people want to be rather than where they are.
Apartments seem to be a good cross section of the population. They cover many socioeconomic conditions, and a variety of population densities. There are plenty of people who are financially capable of buying a house, but choose to rent an apartment. Why? What's so special?
A vibrant neighborhoods are worth checking out. The data-set is inherently biased toward the urban condition with the goal of identifying the outlier. This map makes no assumptions about city or state boundaries, but ends up reinforcing the concept of the metropolitan center. Each city seems to have a hot spot for residential that is distinct from the financial district. In Boston we call it the Back Bay, in New York it's Midtown, Los Angeles has Santa Monica. Padmapper aggregates apartment listings from Craigslist, PadLister, ApartmentSearch, Rent.com, Airbnb, ApartmentFinder and Realtor.com. The listings were downloaded with python based on code forked from Jeff Kaufman
All listings were saved in a .csv file with longitude, latitude, bedroom, price, and date. These values were colored using Photoshop ExtendScript. Here's the Process Book.
